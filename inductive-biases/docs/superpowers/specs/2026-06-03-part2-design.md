## 1. Purpose

Part II takes the second inductive-bias axis, the architecture, and works through four families one at a time. Each chapter states the structural prior the family bakes in, builds the smallest model that isolates it, and checks whether the assumption holds. Convolutional networks assume locality and translation equivariance (Chapter 3). Fixed-context language models assume a bounded window suffices (Chapter 4). Recurrent networks assume time-translation equivariance and squeeze the past through a state bottleneck (Chapter 5). Transformers assume nothing about which positions matter and instead learn a content-addressable lookup (Chapter 6).

Part II is also where the book's third axis appears. Chapter 6 finds that the right architecture is necessary but not sufficient: whether gradient descent realizes the bias-permitted solution depends on positional-encoding scale, initialization, head count, and depth. That is implementation realization, the subject Part III opens on.

Part II is a re-homing of booklet chapters 3 to 6 and their series originals. The prose largely exists; the work is porting it to hand-authored LaTeX, verifying listings against the live library, regenerating numbers and figures from paired notebooks, and removing blog-serialization artifacts (one of which is structural, see Section 3).

## 3. Decisions Settled for Part II

**(A) Chapter order stays LM then RNN; Chapter 4's RNN references are re-aimed forward.** The booklet order is fixed: Chapter 3 CNN, Chapter 4 Fixed-Context LM (Bengio 2003), Chapter 5 RNN, Chapter 6 Transformer. The Chapter 4 source prose was blog-drafted as if the RNN came first ("the RNN in the previous chapter landed at 2.06"; sections "What Bengio gives up / gains vs the RNN"). In book order the RNN is the next chapter. Resolution: Chapter 4 keeps a brief forward preview of the recurrent alternative, and the full Bengio-versus-RNN comparison moves into Chapter 5, which refers back to Chapter 4 naturally (`\Cref`). Rationale: fixed window then recurrent state then attention is one clean "loosen the memory constraint" progression, and Bengio 2003 is the historical entry to neural language models.

**(B) Figures where they add most, generated by the paired notebooks.** The booklet Part II has no figures. Part II adds figures only where they carry the argument: Chapter 6's grokking curve, the M-scaling curve, and the M=32 depth-transition (the chapter's case is visual); the Chapter 3 permuted-pixel comparison; and the Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 loss-trajectory curves. Purely tabular results stay tables. Every figure is generated by the chapter's notebook and placed with `\includegraphics` plus a `\Cref` (Part I lesson S1: generate is not place).

**(C) Pure Python ends at Chapter 6, explicitly.** Chapters 3 to 5 are pure-Python `scratchnn`, model and experiment. Chapter 6's transformer is hand-derived NumPy (`examples/transformer.py`); its headline experiments run on that NumPy model; PyTorch is used only for the M=32 depth and width sweep (`examples/pytorch/`), with the in-text caveat that PyTorch internals differ so its absolute numbers are read only against each other. Chapter 3 already forward-promises this switch; Chapter 6 says it plainly when it arrives.

**(D) Two axes through Chapter 5; the third axis is revealed in Chapter 6.** Chapters 3 to 5 speak of the two axes (output head, architecture), consistent with Part I. Chapter 6 introduces the third (implementation realization) as the synthesis of its scaling investigation. This is the master-spec spine; do not retrofit "three axes" into Chapters 3 to 5. Chapter 3's "inductive bias, named" section may carry a one-line forward hint that realization is coming, no more.

**(E) De-serialization fixes (clear, not decisions).** Remove the surviving "The post is next" and any "post" idiom (Chapter 4 closing especially); fix the stale "the CNN chapter's shapes experiment" in Chapter 5 to "the permuted-pixel control"; reword Chapter 6's pointer to `docs/series/archive/transformer-text.md` (an archived source, not a book chapter); convert blog cross-references to chapter language throughout. Port from the booklet `.tex` (about 90 percent de-bloggified), never from the series `.md` (still full of blog idiom).

## 4. Library State for Part II (verified)

Pure-Python `scratchnn` (standard library only), confirmed in `__init__.py`:

- `Conv2D` (2-D convolution, no padding, stride 1; one kernel per output channel; five-nested-loop forward/backward) and `GlobalAvgPool` (channel-wise mean; turns an equivariant stack into an invariant classifier). Chapter 3 uses `Conv2D`; `GlobalAvgPool` is currently unused (see action items).
- `Embedding` (token-id to vector lookup) and `EmbedConcat` (embed N ids, concatenate). Chapter 4.
- `RNNCell` (vanilla tanh recurrent cell, stateful `forward(x, state)` / `backward(g, dstate_next)` protocol). Chapter 5. No LSTM or GRU class (mentioned, not built).

Chapter 6 uses no `scratchnn`: the transformer is NumPy (`examples/transformer.py`), the large sweep is PyTorch (`examples/pytorch/`).
