\section{Training on 8x8 digits}\label{sec:conv-digits}

We train the convolutional network on the same data and the same loop as the
multilayer perceptron: UCI optdigits (3823 training, 1797 test, ten classes),
pixel values normalized to $[0, 1]$, mini-batch stochastic gradient descent.
Both models use the same hyperparameters, 40 epochs at learning rate 0.1 with
batch size 32, so the comparison is on equal footing. Both finish in a few
minutes of pure Python.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrr@{}}
\toprule
Model & Architecture & Parameters & Test accuracy \\
\midrule
MLP & $64 \to 32 \to 10$ & 2410 & 96.1\% \\
CNN & \texttt{Conv2D(1, 4, k=3)} $\to$ \texttt{ReLU} $\to$ \texttt{Linear(144, 10)}
    & 1490 & 95.4\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The convolutional network is about 38\% smaller in parameter count and lands
within a percentage point of the MLP. It does not dominate. There are two
readings, and they have very different consequences:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The convolutional prior is wrong for this data, so locality does not
  help.
\item The prior is right, but the data has already had locality baked into its
  preprocessing, so asserting locality again gains little.
\end{enumerate}

The second reading is the correct one. The $8 \times 8$ UCI digits are not raw
images. They were produced from the original $32 \times 32$ NIST bitmaps by
counting the ``on'' pixels in each $4 \times 4$ block. That block-counting step
is itself a fixed, translation-invariant local feature extractor: every $8
\times 8$ pixel is already a small local aggregation over a contiguous $4
\times 4$ patch. By the time the convolution sees the data, much of the spatial
work is done, which is why the absolute gap is small. On raw $28 \times 28$
MNIST or on actual photographs the gap between an MLP and a CNN is far larger,
a few percentage points to tens, precisely because the spatial structure has
not been pre-summarized away. To decide between the two readings on this
dataset, rather than argue from the gap, we run an experiment.

\section{The permuted-pixel control}\label{sec:permuted-pixel}

Apply a single fixed random permutation to the 64 input pixels, then train and
test both models on the permuted version. For a human the result is
unreadable: the digits are scrambled past recognition. For the MLP every pixel
position was interchangeable to begin with, so a relabeling of the coordinates
changes nothing it can see, and its accuracy should be essentially unchanged.
For the convolutional network the locality assumption is now \emph{actively
wrong}: neighbors in the permuted input are no longer neighbors in the
original digit, so a $3 \times 3$ kernel reads a meaningless window of
unrelated pixels. The same fixed permutation is applied to both models, under
the same training regime as before.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrr@{}}
\toprule
Model & Pixels & Test accuracy & Drop from standard \\
\midrule
MLP ($64\text{-}32\text{-}10$)  & standard & 96.1\% & \\
MLP ($64\text{-}32\text{-}10$)  & permuted & 95.7\% & $-0.45$ pp \\
CNN ($1, 4, k{=}3$)             & standard & 95.4\% & \\
CNN ($1, 4, k{=}3$)             & permuted & 93.9\% & $-1.50$ pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The MLP drops by about 0.45 percentage points. The CNN drops by about 1.50,
more than three times as much (\Cref{fig:permuted-pixel}). That gap is the
falsification test. If the convolution were ignoring its locality prior,
treating the restricted receptive field as a bottleneck it works around rather
than a constraint it exploits, it would drop by the same tiny amount as the
MLP. It does not. The convolution is genuinely using spatial adjacency, and we
can tell because removing the adjacency costs it more.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/ch03-permuted-pixel.pdf}
  \caption{The permuted-pixel control. Test accuracy on standard pixels (blue)
    and after a single fixed random permutation of the 64 inputs (red), for
    the MLP and the CNN. The MLP, which treats pixel positions as
    interchangeable, barely moves ($-0.45$ pp). The CNN, whose kernels assume
    adjacent pixels are related, loses more than three times as much ($-1.50$
    pp). The direction of the gap is the signature of an inductive bias doing
    real work; its small absolute size reflects how much spatial structure the
    $4 \times 4$ block-counting preprocessing has already removed.}
  \label{fig:permuted-pixel}
\end{figure}

This is the experimental signature of an inductive bias in operation. The
\emph{direction} of the gap, the CNN worse off under permutation, confirms the
prior is load-bearing. The \emph{magnitude}, small in absolute terms because
the dataset was pre-pooled, reflects how much spatial structure survived into
the features. The two findings together say more than the headline ``CNN
matches the MLP with fewer parameters'' can on its own: the parameter
efficiency is not an artifact of a thin head, it comes from the conv kernel
actually exploiting adjacency, and when adjacency is destroyed the efficiency
goes with it.
